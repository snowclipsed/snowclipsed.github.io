<h2>Obsessions with Uneasy Questions</h2>

I think about machine consciousness a lot. Chances are if you are here, you've also thought about it in some way or form, trying to resolve the concept in your head. This concept fairly has kept me up for a lot of nights - it almost feels so fundamental for our understanding of nature. So I decided to write about this probing the topic through various scientific and philosophical perspectives. 

This is a series about *The Metal* (machines) and *Me* (Humans). This edition, is the first one in $n$ others.

<h2>Sometimes Curiousity Doesn't Kill The Cat</h2>

Ever wondered why we ask such big questions? What's something about them which completely *baffles* us and intrigues us? On most nights we're unbothered by big questions, caught up in our daily robotic humdrum. Our brains are geared towards *attention*, we usually tend to focus on a few things at a time. But in those specific moments when we have the time to think for ourselves, to let our brains have free reign - and let our minds wander. 

We're curious beings who seek to understand everything in some way or form. Our overall function as a society and our advancement today is built around a *natural scientific method* - questioning everything, directly or indirectly as a species, then refining the broken, seeking answers for the missing. However, it's not perfect and nowhere as robust as the modern scientific method (case being, we don't know what's the perfect way, we only know the best way). This process of probing may extend to an extent where we are bothered so much by big and unanswered questions, that we try to fill in the voids in our understanding with something I like to call *effective ignorance* - putting a stopper on the most important and inviting questions with misinformation and epistemic closure (believing one's knowledge about a subject is complete and denial for other perspectives or searches for new ideas).

Humans like to seek beauty and purpose in everything they experience or think about. Personally, it feels easier on the mind to digest something if it is beautiful. <h2>(Not) A Race Against Time</h2>

Entropy is a concept wildly misunderstood by the layman, yet it governs us all. In some way, it's what enables us and ends us, what drives societal progress and complexity but also inflation, and crime. Most importantly, it may be embedded in the nature of the universe itself. 

<h3> Is It All The Same?</h3>

> "Entropy is the degree of disorder in a system"

You might have come across this definition of entropy in high school. A single viewpoint to look at entropy does not do it vast topic justice, because it affects everything. The second law of thermodynamics says that disorder increases in any closed system. Information theory dictates that entropy tells us about the degree of uncertainty about a specific event or a set of data. If all the outcomes are equally likely, the entropy of the system is high, and if some outcomes are more likely than others, the outcome is low. But what does this all mean?

<h3>The Entropy Of Tea</h3>

Suppose you're making tea. The first step you take is finding your ingredients - water, sugar and tea leaves. All these ingredients are stored in their own respective jars - they're segregated and unmixed. However, as soon as we mix these ingredients, the border between these ingredients becomes blurry, and they become homogenous and chaotic. They assimilate into a new object, tea. Creation from materials likewise, (usually) requires the increase of entropy. Similarly, when your room is more neatly organized, the objects in the room are more segregated. When your room is messy, they are more disordered and hence more randomly placed. An unorganized room has more entropy.

Again, entropy is also related with the amount of information. The higher the entropy of a certain state, the higher the probability of the state occurring. If the entropy of the entire system is high, this means all the states are equally likely to occur, and hence we gain no information about the states or their likelihood to occur - every time we try to test the state of the system, it is *random*. Coming back to tea, let's assume we want to know whether the tea inside the pot is hot or cold. Initially we have no information on the tea's temperature,  it may be hot or cold. Hence when we decide to pour the tea into a cup, we gain information that was previously unknown to us - the tea is bellowing steam, which may mean that the tea is hot. Now after seeing and feeling the steam through our senses, we gain information about the possible states of tea. Through this, we can make an educated guess in certainty that the tea is hot. We take a sip of the tea, and realize that the tea is less hotter - and slowly gather more and more information about the tea's temperature to make better guesses. Your guesses become better and better - the informational entropy of this tea-and-you system is increasing.

<h3> Time's End </h3>

Time and entropy are in a relentless love spiral towards death and doom, or the heat death of the universe. Since the entropy in the universe is increasing over time, the universe is becoming more and more homogenous. Eventually, after the last black hole has evaporated, the universe will reach a point of maximum entropy where all the position of all pieces of matter in the entire universe are indistinguishable from each other. After this point is reached, time would not have any significance because everything after this equilibrium is reached will forever be the same, and nothing interesting will ever happen. This somewhat grim possibility is currently the most widely accepted model in physics on how the universe might meet its end. However, according to our best estimates, the heat death is [*quite* far away](https://www.technoarete.org/common_abstract/pdf/IJERMCE/v4/i11/Ext_81294.pdf). 

Entropy on its own, is not directly linked to time. This is because entropy is a state function independent of time. Entropy however, increases *with* time, according to the second law of thermodynamics. The increase in entropy being tied with the forward nature of time is often called as the "arrow of time". However, the existence of this arrow is still debated amongst the scientific and philosophical world. Primarily, it is because natural laws follow time symmetry, they act the same way whether time moves backwards or forwards.

<h3> "Because Thou Art Dust...</h3>

Over millennia, we've searched for ways to describe life, and understand it. It's another one of those "big questions" that plague our mind, and hence has been the center of scientific debate. Perhaps, it's also one of those questions which does not have a single answer, but more so can be described by its properties. We get so used to being around living things and consciousness that we sometimes forget how wonderful life truly is. You see, living things have a unique property - they can locally reduce and stabilize their entropy (the entropy of their body and sometimes, immediate surroundings) by expelling heat to the environment in exchange. This is called homeostasis, the ability to maintain our internal conditions despite changes in the environment. We absorb energy through food and respire to locally counteract the natural increase in entropy, maintain order and stability in our bodies after we do mechanical work - this reduces thermodynamical entropy locally. It has always surprised me how life can arise from simple molecules, and our *fuzzy* existence in time. Our bodies change and grow and develop, our atoms are replaced with new, identical ones - yet we're the same person. Hence, life is a system working against entropy - and not just corporeal existence. We're like islands of light in a dark ocean flowing towards disorder, like stars being born and dying - albeit at a much smaller temporal scale. 

<h3>...And To Dust Shalt Thou Return"</h3>

The dynamic push and pull between entropy and homeostasis is what dictates life and death on most accounts. As we grow older, our ability to keep up this homeostasis gets impaired because our cells wear down and our capability to reproduce them decreases, making our bodies more disorderly and causing internal systems to become more susceptible to breakdown and failure. Our death is when the entropy in our system is at the highest - since it is the breakdown of homeostasis or the living system. After our death, the decomposition of our organic matter is also governed by entropy. Our complex bodies are broken down into simpler molecules by decomposers. 

In hindsight, the life-cycle of stars isn't that different from ours. In the modern era, a typical child has a cheerful, explosive birth that ushers emotion in anyone in near vicinity. As children, we conglomerate experiences that live luminously in our minds as memories. Slowly aging towards and beyond adulthood, we start leading more stable lives, shining brightly at the same pace, maybe even settling down in a binary dance with other stars and having planets of our own. Life feels the same for a long time till suddenly one day it's a tad bit harder to get up from the bed. This feeling compounds over time, as we age and worries surmount. We impart as much of ourselves as we can till it's finally time. Some people go out with a bang, others fade to dust from which new life arises - both equally beautiful.
