I am a big fanatic for plant photography, and it's open season when it's raining - capturing the glint of raindrops on fresh leaves in your camera roll is a joy unmatched. It's monsoon lately here in India, and it's been raining at a record level in my city. In the small window when the downpour seizes I like to tread a bit for walks. 

Today was one such morning. I tied up my still-dirty laces from the grocery trip last night and shook off the mud clinging to my shoes on the doormat, embarking off to a park right across the road in front of my house. I shrugged off the unpleasant saturation of humidity from transpiring leaves in the 8 am sun, but in the midst of this, something beautiful caught my eye :

![](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/snowclipsed/snowclipsed.github.io/master/assets/images/hibiscus.jpeg)

A white hibiscus, with a beautifully painted red center. Everytime I see plants which really catch my eye, I usually hop onto google lens *(bless computer vision)* and try to understand what species it is from. A couple of web searches later (while still standing in the middle of the park), I'm informed that hibiscus is actually not a very big genus of plants under the family *Malvaceae*. And this, in particular, is the Hawaiian Dot Hibiscus. Since you can cross-pollinate Hibiscuses, there's various hybrids with different petal pigmentations developed over the years. Another fun fact - the child Hibiscus may not have any shared characteristic from the parent at all! This is because of a condition called [polyploidy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyploidy) where a species has two or more than two complete sets of chromosomes causing a very randomized combinination of selected genes in the offspring, resulting in extremely differing and equally random pigment expressions. This technique is used to produce a lot of hybrid Hibiscus variations, and also to modify fruits and vegetables.

Here's a picture of another beautiful, but red, Chinese Hibiscus.

![](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/snowclipsed/snowclipsed.github.io/master/assets/images/hibiscus_red.jpeg)

You might note that the former just looks like everything but the center was drained of red from the latter - all because of changes in a few genes. That made me realise that genetic expression truly is the most robust and modular language on earth, and every form of life speaks it. Our genetic code, or the way our DNA is sequenced to carry information on how to make proteins, essentially defines how we grow and function. It is the fundamental script of living architectures. Our connection to this script, is one the most beautiful stories. It is a neverending and infinitely complex tale that runs miles through every individual's cells. Essentially, we're evolving code, we're macro molecular automata.
