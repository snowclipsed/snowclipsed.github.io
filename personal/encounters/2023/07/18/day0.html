Day 0 - Applications are hard.

A preface of thoughts about dealing with a sea of paperwork in a middle class family.

Tue, Jul 18, 23 About 7 mins

#personal

Application-Event Theory

Applying for things, is hard (at least for me). At first glance one might positively dismiss this claim as simply “the pressure being too great” - but to me, that dismissal is much more of an insult to whatever tediousness applications can bring with them. It’s a different kind of pressure. Through observation, one can dissect the intriguing phenomenon at play here: the pressure induced by the event of applying, may in part be isolated from the main event that results from that application. Simply put, you can apply for different things (events) say an exam. Signing up for an exam – that’s the application part, like lighting the fuse. But the real deal, the main event we care about, is the exam itself – it’s like the big explosion at the end. I felt like Oppenheimer and my life his bomb - his dreamchild in and out of his hands, but he often didn’t know.

A mandatory "Oppenheimer stressing out" image.

Depending on what the application is for, the act of applying can make a singular event take over my daily life for N amount of days - every waking moment is anxious with different possibilities of how the event may turn out. Every minute is identified as a countdown. Every day’s end is sprinkled with the bitterness of procrastination. The more life changing the event is, the more intense this effect, almost exponentially growing till it ultimately transforms into dread during the climactic days. This is even more accentuated if you know when you’re applying, causing the application to recursively become its own event, coupling together an added pressure of applying to something properly. Time gated forms make this worse, and those which clear out your information and reset you back to the start are downright evil. It’s like playing a roguelike game except without the thrill of the unknown but more so the dread - unless of course, you like filling forms. But, I know it isn’t just me who dreads this process. After all, most people are pale-faced before large events in their lives, and I have experienced such events multiple times with my peers now to know that. Everyone likes to wear a ski mask of painted emotions on their faces come judgement day - but the thing about ski masks is that they don’t cover one’s eyes and mouths. Eyes tell a story, so do words - and I’ve learned that in many ways. So, suffering alone, bad. Suffering in a group of approximately equally application-drained individuals… less bad. It’s somewhat comforting, depending on how you want to look at it. I usually choose to let it be comforting, and the comfort is maximized when you talk to others to share your anxieties in hope to mutually dilute it in a pool of words.

A Recent Experience

I went through such this nerve wracking phase in the last month, and a really important one at that. And I suppose every visa aspirant and student looking to restart their life overseas has experienced this first hand. In the sea of forms that we had to take care of also lied my entire family’s future. I am one of the lucky ones to be sponsored by my parents to study abroad, and for that, I am very grateful to them. However, the exchange mentality that we have always lived in, where every act of conversation is just an instance of selling oneself and one’s availability and services to another - being funded by your loved ones falls into a very cornered, morally gray area when you think about how to give back to them. In some sense, you never can - the support provided can be never paid back because it basically has the potential to kickstart a person’s life. Experiences in foreign lands change things. However, as many spout - it is simply not as unconditional as it seems to be. Every Indian MS aspirant has to some degree, experienced a counter pressure of giving back to their parents. To some, it may appear as the logical extension of moral Indian values to care for one’s parents. For others, it may come across as a excessively restrictive mentality. I think I lie somewhere in the middle, but eventually I want a better future for my parents. I think they’re talented, and they too - deserve a chance. It gives me comfort to believe, even to a hopeless extent that it’s never too late to start a venture - think Col. Sanders and Samuel L. Jackson’s success stories.

(Approximately) equivalent exchange. Edward Elric is a salesman, and so are we.

When Yellow Dandelions Turn Grey

There’s a lot of life still left in my past-middle age parents. I’ve always fought with them as a kid, teen, and adult - but the gray in their hair is more noticeable than ever. I don’t talk to them deeply as much, it’s usually my mother scolding me over chores - you know - the typical Indian middle class household. But when I do, a small part of my mind deep inside is often moved to tears interacting with a human who does care about me. I still do not regret being angry when I was angry, and frustrated when I was frustrated - but I do respect them.

I talked to my dad recently. Both of us were not able to sleep when booking for the visa appointment, and in the pitter-patter of rain and future plans, I posed him a question I never thought I’d pose - “Where do you want to travel?”. The look on his face was bewildered at first, then a strange mute as if almost forcefully trying to hide his smile. He ended up smiling anyway, causing me to sit up. It’s a rare occurrence for me to make them smile so directly, so that was great. I got my answer as well, so I will be looking forward to funding them a trip to Europe someday. Today, I am happy to have them as my parents and I would want nobody else, maybe tomorrow will be different - but that’s okay. In some ways, this is a letter to the future me to have faith in them and me.

That’s all for the first post in this series! In the next post (Day 1), I’ll talk more about my experience being a recent US F-1 visa applicant - stay tuned.
